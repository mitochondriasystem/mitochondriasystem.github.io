I snapped, catching my breath, trembling at the thought of the horror coming true. 

My breathing calms and the sound of crickets vanquishes the silence of the night. Figures formed shadows on the wall as I turned the lights on to verify my dreams untrue—that they were just nightmares an adult gets at least once. Yet the truth unfolds. There was only a pillow, no indications of a body lying beside mine. Not even a tangle on the duvet.

All of my rationality diminished in disgruntledness, hence I saw my indignant self storming the kitchen, the bathroom, and even the alleys of our floor—expecting to see her dancing to the tune of an old hymn, secretly sneaking a tub of rocky road ice cream, or even coming out of the elevator. I would find her and she would tell me that it was all a dream. As always.

I bolted awake, hastening to scream her name only to utter none.

My feet strode on their own towards the balcony, meandering. It was as if such had become a mannerism of mine each time I wake from that dream. The ashtray stood still above the barricades of our balcony. Lingering on the thought, I plucked a stick of cigarette inside my pocket. Flickering the lighter, the cigarette touched my lips and I couldn't get enough. The filthy smoke seems to ghost a picture like a molding clay of clouds in the murkiest of night. Seizing my time to breathe out, the emanating icy blast flavor blocks my sight of the glimmering ball of gas. That is when I felt that I needed to stop looking for her.

That is how the wind shattered my words away.

—

It was quite dark but not so long before the sun rose, I decided to jog around to dismiss the unwanted thoughts occupying my mind. Covered in sweat, the burning sensation begins to spread across my limbs. The houses disappeared in the fog until they were out of sight when I passed by the Conor bridge. I paused at an old wooden bench under a lamp post. The sun peaks on the horizon, gracing the forest with daylight. I settled down, disparaging the picturesque scene.

My back stung from the first scar I got when I was 14. It was under the sky of October when my father and I giggled at the uncanny snowman.

My right foot slid and had a collision.

The next thing I saw was the snow, drenched with his blood. I was lingering on the verge of despair when she grabbed my hand and shed light on my darkest time. But October came and the sky was no different from that day.

I was reminded that in the end, we remain underdogs of death.

—

Fifteen minutes have passed, and I have no idea why my feet have brought me here. A white gate stood in front of me, towering eerily as the trees cast shade against the peak of the sun. I rotated the gate latch but it wouldn't budge.

"Don't try, it's locked inside."

I spun around, surprised by the voice behind me.

"Follow me," a guy my age said before disappearing into the woods, his boots crunching on fallen leaves. 

"I've been here quite a lot for burying bodies," he says, provocatively raising his eyebrows.

What a portentous moment, I thought.

We parted ways as we crossed the statue of Angelus without bidding goodbye. Rude, but I will not thank a man who informs illicitly.

This had been the gathering area of the soldiers who perished from battling death. Humans weren't meant to be immortal, so when death approaches—go along. Don't escape, don't run away. You can steal death, but at the cost of it coming after your loved ones.

Locking my eyes straight ahead as though I knew where to go, I found her in the heart of the forest—secluded from any other grave. Every step towards her torments my soul. My knees have lost their strength, stooping me down on the frigid dusty floor. Mourning.

I presumed the wind wouldn't blow more.

Yellow undertones lined the walls, anticipating the slight dances my eyes conjure. The entirety of the experience speaks of several pictures shelved inside my head—those of my childhood and my growing years. They flicker instantaneously, like a soothing scene from a coming of age film—gradually, until nothing is left to watch.

Miserably, I found her.
